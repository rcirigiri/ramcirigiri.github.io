Case Study · Cogo Order

Building a three-channel conversational ordering system

● In production across 5 restaurants  ·  Patent-pending (USPTO #64/026,827)

Cogo lets restaurant customers place an order by talking or typing — across in-store voice, the phone, and chat — and completes most orders in a single back-and-forth. This is how it's built, the decisions that mattered, and what they bought.

The problem

Restaurants lose orders and staff time to manual phone and counter ordering, and the off-the-shelf voice systems they can buy fall apart on the two things that matter most in a real dining room: accented and multilingual speech, and noisy environments. An ordering assistant that mishears "no cilantro" or a menu item half the time is worse than no assistant at all. The goal was an assistant accurate enough to trust with a live order, fast enough to feel like a conversation, and cheap enough to run on restaurant margins.

Constraints

  • Accuracy on accented and multilingual speech, against each restaurant's specific menu vocabulary.
  • Conversational latency — responses fast enough that talking to it feels natural.
  • Cost — running a frontier model on every turn is a non-starter at restaurant economics.
  • Reliability & compliance — graceful failover, plus PII handling and audit trails on a system touching customer data.
  • Multi-tenant — every restaurant has its own menu, modifiers, and pricing; the same agent has to adapt to each.

Architecture

Order flow — from customer turn to completed order, with cross-cutting reliability concerns.

Key decisions

01

Run two speech engines and arbitrate, instead of trusting one

Voice and phone orders run two independent ASR engines in parallel. A lightweight arbitration layer scores both transcripts against the restaurant's menu vocabulary and selects the more accurate one in about a millisecond, which sharply reduces mis-heard items on accented and multilingual speech.

Tradeoff: two engines roughly double speech-to-text cost per call — but a single engine mis-hearing menu items is the failure that makes customers abandon the assistant, so the accuracy was worth the spend. The method is patent-pending.

02

Structure the agent as a state machine with grounded tools

Rather than a single free-form prompt, the ordering agent is an 18-node LangGraph state machine with 32 grounded tools — menu lookup, cart operations, pricing, modifiers, allergen checks, recommendations. Grounding every action in the restaurant's live menu is what lets most orders complete in a single exchange and keeps the agent's behavior predictable.

Tradeoff: more upfront engineering than a prompt, but predictable, testable behavior — which is what made a 630-test regression suite possible in the first place.

03

Route most turns to a cheap model, escalate only when needed

A deterministic fast-path handles the most common patterns without an LLM at all, and tiered routing sends roughly 70% of remaining turns to a low-cost model, escalating to a stronger one only when the turn warrants it.

Tradeoff: routing logic to maintain, but it cut about a second of latency per turn and kept serving cost low with no measurable quality loss on our evaluation set.

04

Cache aggressively in two tiers

An in-memory L1 plus a Redis L2 cache absorbs the repeated work — menu reads, common lookups — at an 80%+ hit rate, which keeps infrastructure to roughly $500/month at current scale: about 1% of what a naive "call the LLM for everything" design would cost.

Tradeoff: cache-invalidation discipline when menus change — handled by tying cache keys to menu versioning.

Most orders now complete in a single exchange. The system has been built greenfield to production, and a sister product — AI grocery product-discovery — is in pilot with 2 stores, reusing the same grounding and routing patterns.

Reliability & compliance

Because it touches live customer orders, the system was engineered for production from the start: failover across both speech and LLM providers, per-session distributed locking, PII redaction and audit logging on every model call, p95/error-rate observability, and the 630-test CI-gated regression suite that guards every deploy.

Scaling & what's next

The canonical-normalization architecture behind the menu grounding is designed to scale to 10,000 restaurants — the current 5 are real production traffic on infrastructure built for far more. Next is deeper per-customer personalization on the ordering side, and graduating the grocery product-discovery system from pilot toward production. The work was delivered by a cross-functional team of 2–4 engineers across product, data science, and infrastructure.
